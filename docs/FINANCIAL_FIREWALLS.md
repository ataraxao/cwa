# Financial Separation & Commingling Avoidance (Implementation Notes)
*Conceptual template. Not legal, tax, or financial advice. Not part of the Agreement.*

## Purpose
These notes are an implementation-oriented checklist to help keep the **facts on the ground** consistent with a wealth-only, ring-fenced, event-driven governance mechanism. They are intended to reduce avoidable ambiguity that can arise from sloppy commingling, unclear titling, or informal money flows.

**Not incorporated.** This document is not incorporated into the Cooperative Wealth Governance Agreement (the “Agreement”) unless the Agreement expressly states so in a signed writing that specifically identifies this document.

---

## A. Minimal viable firewall (default posture)
If the Parties share a residence (or otherwise share daily life), the cleanest default is:

1) **Vehicle Account = wealth-only**
- The Vehicle Account is used only for:
  - investing under guardrails,
  - Administrator Events (Liquidity Events, termination unwind, dispute holdbacks/releases),
  - administrator fees and required withholding/charges.
- **Avoid** using the Vehicle Account to pay recurring living expenses (rent/mortgage, utilities, groceries, travel, insurance, childcare, etc.).

2) **Lifestyle spending stays outside the Vehicle**
- If one Party chooses to pay day-to-day expenses, do it from a separate personal checking account in that Party’s name (or a separately titled, clearly defined household spending account—see Section B).

3) **No joint debt by default**
- Avoid joint credit cards, joint personal loans, joint auto loans, or any co-signed obligations unless there is a specific written agreement describing responsibility and exit handling.

4) **No joint brokerage / joint investment accounts**
- Keep investment accounts separate unless they are:
  - the Vehicle Account (restricted), or
  - expressly structured and documented as a Joint Asset purchase under the Agreement.

5) **Title matches intent**
- Real property, vehicles, and major assets should be titled consistently with the intended ownership:
  - Separate property remains titled separately.
  - Shared economic intent should use the Agreement’s Joint Asset protocol or a separate co-ownership document.

## B. Household spending: two clean options
If the Parties live together, recurring expenses create the most “fact-pattern risk” when they are informal, inconsistent, or appear like reliance-based support.

### Option 1 (simplest): One-Payer Voluntary Spending
- One Party pays most shared expenses from **their personal account**.
- The other Party pays nothing or pays specific categories directly (their phone, their car, etc.).
- No reimbursements unless a written, one-time agreement exists for that specific item.

**Best for simplicity.** Facts stay simple: “voluntary spending, no reimbursement expectation.”

### Option 2 (clearer accounting): A Dedicated Household Spending Account
If you want clean visibility without mixing with the Vehicle:

- Open a separate checking account used only for defined household categories.
- Fund it via periodic voluntary deposits.
- Keep the account statement history.

**Implementation rules:**
- Do not title this account to the Vehicle LLC.
- Avoid treating it as an “entitlement account.”
- Avoid language like “support,” “maintenance,” “allowance,” or “owed.”

**Note:** If you use this option, keep it purely operational. Avoid turning it into a shadow contract.

## C. Red flags (avoid these patterns)
These are common fact patterns that tend to create ambiguity later:

1) **Vehicle Account pays lifestyle**
- Regular transfers from the Vehicle Account to cover living expenses.
- Paying one Party’s recurring personal expenses directly from the Vehicle.

2) **Informal reimbursement expectations**
- “I’ll pay now, you’ll settle up later” without any documentation.
- Accumulating “tabs” or implied debts.

3) **Major upgrades to separate property without a signed deal**
- One Party spends significant personal money improving the other Party’s separately titled home.
- Then later claims “equity” or “reimbursement” based on the spend.

4) **Ambiguous titles**
- Putting both names on assets “for convenience” without a clear ownership agreement.

5) **Co-signed obligations without exit planning**
- Joint leases, co-signed loans, shared credit lines that create implied shared economics.

---

## D. Property improvements (separate property stays separate)
If one Party owns a home or other major separate property:

- Avoid major improvements paid by the non-owner unless there is a **contemporaneous signed writing** that:
  1) identifies the property,
  2) states whether it creates ownership or reimbursement,
  3) defines the valuation method and timing.

**Default behavior:** If there is no signed writing, treat improvement spending as voluntary and non-reimbursable, and avoid making the expenditure.

---

## E. Documentation hygiene (low-friction, non-relationship framing)
If the Parties want minimal documentation for clarity, keep it operational and narrow:

- Keep copies of:
  - major receipts for Joint Asset purchases,
  - titles and ownership percentages (if applicable),
  - any one-time written agreements about reimbursements or ownership.

**Avoid:** “relationship” memos, expectations, duties, or ongoing promises. Keep it about ownership, accounting, and mechanics.

---

## F. What to tell professionals reviewing the structure (one-paragraph framing)
> The Agreement is intended to govern a restricted wealth vehicle only. To keep the factual record clean, the Vehicle Account is not used for lifestyle spending, the Parties avoid joint debt and joint brokerage accounts, and any shared ownership intent is implemented via explicit titling and written documentation (Joint Asset protocol or a separate co-ownership instrument).
